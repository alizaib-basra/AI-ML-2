========================================
ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE - OVERVIEW
========================================

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the simulation of human intelligence processes by computer systems. These processes include learning (acquiring information and rules for using it), reasoning (using rules to reach approximate or definite conclusions), and self-correction.

AI was founded as an academic discipline in 1956 at the Dartmouth Conference, where the term was coined by John McCarthy. Early AI research focused on symbolic methods and problem-solving. In the 1980s, machine learning became popular, followed by deep learning in the 2010s.

Major branches of AI include:
- Machine Learning: Systems that learn from data without being explicitly programmed
- Natural Language Processing (NLP): Enabling computers to understand human language
- Computer Vision: Teaching machines to interpret visual information
- Robotics: AI-powered physical machines that interact with the environment
- Expert Systems: Programs that mimic human expert decision-making

========================================
MACHINE LEARNING
========================================

Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI that provides systems the ability to automatically learn and improve from experience without being explicitly programmed. ML focuses on developing computer programs that can access data and use it to learn for themselves.

Types of Machine Learning:
1. Supervised Learning: The algorithm learns from labeled training data. Examples include classification and regression tasks. Common algorithms: Linear Regression, Decision Trees, Random Forest, Support Vector Machines (SVM), Neural Networks.

2. Unsupervised Learning: The algorithm finds patterns in data without labeled responses. Examples include clustering and dimensionality reduction. Common algorithms: K-Means, DBSCAN, Principal Component Analysis (PCA), Autoencoders.

3. Reinforcement Learning: The algorithm learns by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards or penalties. Used in game playing (AlphaGo), robotics, and recommendation systems.

4. Semi-supervised Learning: Uses a small amount of labeled data and a large amount of unlabeled data for training.

Key ML concepts:
- Training Data: The dataset used to train the model
- Test Data: Data used to evaluate model performance
- Overfitting: When a model performs well on training data but poorly on new data
- Underfitting: When a model is too simple to capture the underlying pattern
- Cross-validation: Technique to assess model generalization
- Hyperparameter Tuning: Optimizing model configuration parameters

========================================
DEEP LEARNING
========================================

Deep Learning is a subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with many layers (deep neural networks) to model complex patterns in data. It has revolutionized fields like image recognition, speech processing, and natural language understanding.

Key Deep Learning architectures:
1. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN): Specialized for image and video processing. Used in image classification, object detection, facial recognition.

2. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN): Designed for sequential data like text and time series. LSTMs and GRUs are improved variants that handle long-term dependencies.

3. Transformers: Architecture based on self-attention mechanisms. Revolutionized NLP. Examples: BERT, GPT, T5.

4. Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN): Two networks compete — a generator creates fake data, a discriminator tries to detect fakes. Used for image generation, style transfer.

5. Autoencoders: Learn compressed representations of data. Used for anomaly detection, dimensionality reduction, and generative modeling.

Deep Learning breakthroughs:
- 2012: AlexNet wins ImageNet — deep learning enters mainstream
- 2014: GANs introduced by Ian Goodfellow
- 2017: Transformer architecture introduced ("Attention is All You Need")
- 2018: BERT revolutionizes NLP
- 2020: GPT-3 shows emergent capabilities in language models
- 2022: ChatGPT brings conversational AI to mainstream users
- 2023: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini advance multimodal capabilities

========================================
NATURAL LANGUAGE PROCESSING
========================================

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a branch of AI that deals with the interaction between computers and humans using natural language. The ultimate goal is to enable computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language.

Core NLP tasks:
- Tokenization: Breaking text into words or subwords
- Part-of-Speech Tagging: Identifying grammatical roles of words
- Named Entity Recognition (NER): Identifying people, places, organizations
- Sentiment Analysis: Determining emotional tone of text
- Machine Translation: Translating between languages
- Text Summarization: Condensing long documents
- Question Answering: Responding to questions based on context
- Text Generation: Producing coherent human-like text

Key NLP models and tools:
- BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers): Google's model for understanding context in both directions
- GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer): OpenAI's autoregressive language model
- spaCy: Industrial-strength NLP library in Python
- NLTK: Natural Language Toolkit for Python
- Hugging Face Transformers: Library providing thousands of pre-trained models

========================================
LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS (LLMs)
========================================

Large Language Models are AI systems trained on massive text datasets to understand and generate human language. They use transformer architecture and are characterized by billions of parameters.

Notable LLMs:
- GPT-4 (OpenAI): Multimodal model capable of text and image understanding
- Claude (Anthropic): Known for safety, helpfulness, and long context windows
- Gemini (Google): Natively multimodal model from Google DeepMind
- Llama (Meta): Open-source family of language models
- Mistral: Efficient open-source models from French startup Mistral AI

LLM capabilities:
- Text generation and completion
- Question answering and summarization
- Code generation and debugging
- Translation and language understanding
- Reasoning and problem solving
- Creative writing and ideation

LLM limitations:
- Hallucination: Generating plausible but incorrect information
- Knowledge cutoff: No awareness of events after training date
- Context window limits: Cannot process infinitely long documents
- Bias: Reflecting biases present in training data
- Computational cost: Require significant compute for inference

========================================
RETRIEVAL AUGMENTED GENERATION (RAG)
========================================

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that enhances LLM responses by retrieving relevant information from external knowledge bases before generating answers. It combines the power of retrieval systems with generative AI.

How RAG works:
1. Document Ingestion: Documents are loaded and split into chunks
2. Embedding: Text chunks are converted to vector embeddings
3. Vector Storage: Embeddings stored in a vector database
4. Query Processing: User query is embedded into the same vector space
5. Retrieval: Most similar document chunks are retrieved
6. Generation: LLM generates answer using retrieved context + query

RAG advantages over fine-tuning:
- No expensive retraining required
- Knowledge can be updated without retraining
- Sources can be cited for transparency
- Reduces hallucination by grounding responses in documents
- Can work with private/proprietary data

Popular RAG frameworks:
- LangChain: Comprehensive framework for LLM applications
- LlamaIndex: Data framework for LLM applications
- Haystack: NLP framework with strong retrieval capabilities

Vector databases for RAG:
- Chroma: Lightweight, open-source, easy to use locally
- Pinecone: Managed cloud vector database
- Weaviate: Open-source vector search engine
- FAISS: Facebook AI Similarity Search library
- Qdrant: High-performance vector search engine

========================================
LANGCHAIN FRAMEWORK
========================================

LangChain is a framework for developing applications powered by large language models. It provides tools and abstractions for building context-aware, reasoning applications.

Core LangChain components:
- Models: Interfaces to LLMs and chat models
- Prompts: Prompt templates and management
- Chains: Sequences of calls to models or utilities
- Memory: Storing and retrieving conversation history
- Agents: LLMs that can use tools to accomplish tasks
- Document Loaders: Loading data from various sources
- Text Splitters: Breaking documents into manageable chunks
- Vector Stores: Storing and searching document embeddings
- Retrievers: Fetching relevant documents

LangChain memory types:
- ConversationBufferMemory: Stores all conversation history
- ConversationBufferWindowMemory: Keeps last K interactions
- ConversationSummaryMemory: Summarizes older conversations
- ConversationSummaryBufferMemory: Hybrid approach
- VectorStoreRetrieverMemory: Stores memories in vector store

========================================
VECTOR EMBEDDINGS
========================================

Vector embeddings are numerical representations of data (text, images, audio) in a high-dimensional space where semantically similar items are positioned close together.

How embeddings work:
- Text is converted to a fixed-size vector of floating point numbers
- Similar meanings result in vectors that are close in vector space
- Distance metrics (cosine similarity, euclidean distance) measure similarity
- Embeddings capture semantic meaning, not just keywords

Popular embedding models:
- OpenAI text-embedding-ada-002: High quality, widely used
- Sentence Transformers: Open-source, runs locally
- Cohere Embed: Commercial embedding API
- all-MiniLM-L6-v2: Fast, lightweight sentence transformer

Applications of embeddings:
- Semantic search
- Document similarity
- Recommendation systems
- Clustering and classification
- RAG knowledge retrieval

========================================
AI ETHICS AND SAFETY
========================================

AI ethics refers to the moral principles and techniques employed to inform the development and responsible use of artificial intelligence technology.

Key ethical concerns:
- Bias and Fairness: AI systems can perpetuate or amplify existing societal biases
- Privacy: AI systems often require large amounts of personal data
- Transparency: Many AI models are "black boxes" with unexplainable decisions
- Accountability: Determining responsibility when AI causes harm
- Job displacement: Automation replacing human workers
- Misinformation: AI generating convincing false content

AI Safety research areas:
- Alignment: Ensuring AI systems pursue intended goals
- Interpretability: Understanding how AI models make decisions
- Robustness: Making AI reliable in adversarial conditions
- Constitutional AI: Teaching AI to follow principles (Anthropic's approach)
- RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

Major AI safety organizations:
- Anthropic: Safety-focused AI company, created Claude
- DeepMind Safety: Research division at Google DeepMind
- Center for Human-Compatible AI (CHAI): UC Berkeley research center
- Machine Intelligence Research Institute (MIRI): Non-profit AI safety research

========================================
COMPUTER VISION
========================================

Computer Vision is a field of AI that trains computers to interpret and understand visual information from the world, such as images and videos.

Core computer vision tasks:
- Image Classification: Assigning a label to an entire image
- Object Detection: Locating and classifying objects in images
- Image Segmentation: Classifying each pixel in an image
- Facial Recognition: Identifying or verifying persons from faces
- Optical Character Recognition (OCR): Extracting text from images
- Pose Estimation: Detecting human body positions

Popular CV frameworks and models:
- YOLO (You Only Look Once): Real-time object detection
- ResNet: Deep residual networks for image classification
- VGG: Deep CNN architecture from Oxford
- EfficientNet: Scalable and efficient CNNs
- OpenCV: Open-source computer vision library
- CLIP (OpenAI): Connecting text and images

Applications:
- Autonomous vehicles (Tesla, Waymo)
- Medical imaging diagnosis
- Security and surveillance
- Augmented reality
- Quality control in manufacturing
- Agricultural monitoring
